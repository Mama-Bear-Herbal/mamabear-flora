Key herbal allies for the gut
Slippery Elm
Ulmus rubra
The inner bark contains mucilage, a gel-forming substance that coats and soothes the gut lining. Clinical research published in 2020 found formulas containing slippery elm significantly improved symptoms of IBS including abdominal pain, bloating, and stool consistency. It may also help reduce intestinal permeability.
Clinical trial evidence
Marshmallow Root
Althaea officinalis
Rich in mucilaginous polysaccharides, marshmallow root has antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. It forms a protective layer along the digestive tract and may help restore the intestinal barrier. Used as a sustainable alternative to slippery elm with similar demulcent actions.
Traditional use · emerging research
Peppermint
Mentha × piperita
Enteric-coated peppermint oil has strong clinical evidence for IBS, with multiple meta-analyses showing superior global symptom reduction versus placebo. It relaxes gastrointestinal smooth muscle via calcium channel blockade, reducing spasm and abdominal pain. Note: not recommended for those with acid reflux.
Strong meta-analysis evidence (IBS)
Chamomile
Matricaria chamomilla
An anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic herb used extensively in Western herbal medicine for the digestive system. Chamomile is a component of Iberogast (STW 5), a well-studied herbal preparation with clinical trial evidence for functional dyspepsia and IBD symptom relief. Also addresses the gut-brain connection through nervine relaxant properties.
Component of clinically-trialled formula
Turmeric
Curcuma longa
Curcumin, the active component of turmeric, has well-documented antioxidant and anti-inflammatory actions. It reduces inflammatory cytokine production and has been investigated in human studies for ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease. Research shows it may help maintain remission as an adjunct to standard care.
Multiple human studies (IBD)
Fennel
Foeniculum vulgare
A carminative herb that reduces bloating, flatulence, and intestinal spasm. Anethole, its primary active compound, has a soothing effect on the digestive tract. Used traditionally and in modern herbal practice for IBS, particularly where gas and cramping are dominant symptoms.
Traditional use · laboratory evidence

IBS
Irritable Bowel Syndrome
1 in 5
people in the UK
What it is
IBS is a chronic disorder of gut-brain interaction characterised by recurring abdominal pain associated with changes in bowel habit. There is no structural damage, but the impact on quality of life can be significant. Research identifies dysbiosis — imbalance in the gut microbiome — as a key associated factor, affecting the intestinal barrier, immune response, and gut-brain signalling.
Common symptoms
Abdominal pain Bloating Diarrhoea Constipation Flatulence Urgency
"Herbal approaches targeting gut motility, mucosal integrity, and the gut-brain axis — including peppermint, slippery elm, and chamomile — have clinical trial evidence supporting their use in IBS symptom management."

IBD
Inflammatory Bowel Disease
500k
people in the UK
What it is
IBD encompasses Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis — chronic inflammatory conditions of the gastrointestinal tract. Research consistently identifies gut microbiome dysbiosis as a central feature, with an expansion of pro-inflammatory bacteria and reduction of protective species such as Faecalibacterium prausnitzii. Herbal medicine is increasingly studied as a complementary approach alongside conventional treatment.
Common symptoms
Persistent diarrhoea Rectal bleeding Abdominal cramps Fatigue Weight loss Urgency
"Natural products including curcumin, Aloe vera, Boswellia, and slippery elm have been investigated for their anti-inflammatory action in IBD, with several human studies showing measurable benefit as adjunctive therapy."

Coeliac Disease
Autoimmune gluten intolerance
1%
of the UK population
What it is
Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition in which gluten triggers an immune response that damages the small intestinal lining, impairing nutrient absorption. A strict gluten-free diet is essential. Many individuals continue to experience persistent symptoms post-diagnosis due to gut mucosal damage, nutritional deficiencies, and ongoing dysbiosis — areas where herbal and nutritional support may offer meaningful benefit.
Common symptoms
Bloating Diarrhoea Fatigue Anaemia Bone pain Mouth ulcers
"Herbal support for coeliac disease focuses on gut mucosal repair, reducing residual inflammation, and addressing nutritional deficits — using demulcent and anti-inflammatory herbs alongside dietary guidance."

Diverticulitis
Inflamed colonic pouches
50%
of over-60s in the West
What it is
Diverticulitis occurs when small pouches (diverticula) that form in the colon become inflamed or infected. Research has identified a dysbiotic pattern — a reduction in anti-inflammatory species including Lactobacilli and Bacteroides — as a contributing factor. Gut motility disruption driven by microbial imbalance is also implicated in symptom development.
Common symptoms
Lower abdominal pain Fever Nausea Bloating Change in bowel habit
"Anti-inflammatory herbal approaches and microbiome-supportive botanicals are being investigated for their role in managing diverticular disease, with a focus on reducing mucosal inflammation and restoring microbial balance."
