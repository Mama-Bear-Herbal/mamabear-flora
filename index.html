Key herbal allies
Slippery Elm
Ulmus rubra
The inner bark contains mucilage — a gel-forming substance that coats and soothes the gut lining. A 2020 clinical study found formulas containing slippery elm significantly improved IBS symptoms including abdominal pain, bloating, and stool consistency. It may also help reduce intestinal permeability.
Clinical trial evidence
Marshmallow Root
Althaea officinalis
Rich in mucilaginous polysaccharides with antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties. Marshmallow root forms a protective coating along the digestive tract and may help restore the intestinal barrier — a sustainable alternative to slippery elm with similar demulcent actions.
Traditional use · emerging research
Peppermint
Mentha × piperita
Enteric-coated peppermint oil has strong clinical evidence for IBS. Multiple meta-analyses show superior global symptom reduction versus placebo. It relaxes gastrointestinal smooth muscle via calcium channel blockade, reducing spasm and abdominal pain. Note: not recommended where acid reflux is present.
Strong meta-analysis evidence · IBS
Chamomile
Matricaria chamomilla
A key anti-inflammatory and antispasmodic herb for the digestive system. Chamomile is a component of Iberogast (STW 5), a well-studied herbal preparation with clinical trial evidence for functional dyspepsia and IBD symptom relief. Its nervine relaxant properties also address the gut-brain connection.
Component of clinically-trialled formula
Turmeric
Curcuma longa
Curcumin, the active component of turmeric, has well-documented antioxidant and anti-inflammatory actions. It reduces inflammatory cytokine production and has been investigated in human studies for both ulcerative colitis and Crohn's disease, showing potential to help maintain remission as an adjunct to standard care.
Multiple human studies · IBD
Fennel
Foeniculum vulgare
A carminative herb that reduces bloating, flatulence, and intestinal spasm. Anethole, its primary active compound, has a soothing effect on the digestive tract. Used traditionally and in modern herbal practice for IBS, particularly where gas and cramping are dominant symptoms.
Traditional use · laboratory evidence

Evidence-based explainers for the conditions we specialise in
IBS
Irritable Bowel Syndrome
1 in 5
people in the UK
What it is
IBS is a chronic disorder of gut-brain interaction characterised by recurring abdominal pain associated with changes in bowel habit. There is no structural damage, but the impact on quality of life can be significant. Research consistently identifies dysbiosis as a key associated factor, affecting the intestinal barrier, immune response, and gut-brain signalling.
Common symptoms
Abdominal pain Bloating Diarrhoea Constipation Flatulence Urgency
Herbal approaches targeting gut motility, mucosal integrity, and the gut-brain axis — including peppermint, slippery elm, and chamomile — have clinical trial evidence supporting their use in IBS symptom management.

IBD
Inflammatory Bowel Disease
500k
people in the UK
What it is
IBD encompasses Crohn's disease and ulcerative colitis — chronic inflammatory conditions of the gastrointestinal tract. Research consistently identifies gut microbiome dysbiosis as a central feature, with an expansion of pro-inflammatory bacteria and reduction of protective species. Herbal medicine is increasingly studied as a complementary approach alongside conventional treatment.
Common symptoms
Persistent diarrhoea Rectal bleeding Abdominal cramps Fatigue Weight loss
Natural products including curcumin, Aloe vera, Boswellia, and slippery elm have been investigated for their anti-inflammatory action in IBD, with several human studies showing measurable benefit as adjunctive therapy.

Coeliac Disease
Autoimmune gluten intolerance
1%
of the UK population
What it is
Coeliac disease is an autoimmune condition in which gluten triggers an immune response that damages the small intestinal lining, impairing nutrient absorption. A strict gluten-free diet is essential. Many individuals continue to experience persistent symptoms post-diagnosis due to gut mucosal damage, nutritional deficiencies, and ongoing dysbiosis — areas where herbal and nutritional support may offer meaningful benefit.
Common symptoms
Bloating Diarrhoea Fatigue Anaemia Bone pain Mouth ulcers
Herbal support for coeliac disease focuses on gut mucosal repair, reducing residual inflammation, and addressing nutritional deficits — using demulcent and anti-inflammatory herbs alongside dietary guidance.

Diverticulitis
Inflamed colonic pouches
50%
of over-60s in the West
What it is
Diverticulitis occurs when small pouches that form in the colon become inflamed or infected. Research has identified a dysbiotic pattern — a reduction in anti-inflammatory species including Lactobacilli and Bacteroides — as a contributing factor. Gut motility disruption driven by microbial imbalance is also implicated in symptom development.
Common symptoms
Lower abdominal pain Fever Nausea Bloating Change in bowel habit
Anti-inflammatory herbal approaches and microbiome-supportive botanicals are being investigated for their role in managing diverticular disease, with a focus on reducing mucosal inflammation and restoring microbial balance.
